## Part 3: Strategic Prompting Techniques
### 3.1 Chain-of-Thought Prompting:

* **Chosen Problem:** Option C: Planning Task

#### Without Chain-of-Thought
* **Prompt:** You have a meeting at 2 PM that requires 1 hour of prep time,
a 30-minute commute, and you need to eat lunch (30 mins) beforehand.
You also have a 45-minute call scheduled at 11 AM. What time should you
start preparing to make everything work?just provide me the final time.
* **LLM Output:**

  > 12:00 PM

#### With Chain-of-Thought
* **Prompt:** You have a meeting at 2 PM that requires 1 hour of prep time,
a 30-minute commute, and you need to eat lunch (30 mins) beforehand.
You also have a 45-minute call scheduled at 11 AM. What time should you
start preparing to make everything work?let us solve this step by step
* **LLM Output with Reasoning:**
>   Let's solve this step by step, working backward from your 2:00 PM meeting to find the latest possible time you can start preparing.
>
>**Step 1: Identify the final anchor point**
>
>* **Event:** Main Meeting
>* **Start Time:** 2:00 PM
>
>**Step 2: Subtract the commute time**
You need a 30-minute commute to get to the meeting on time.
>
>* 2:00 PM minus 30 minutes = **1:30 PM** (This is when you must leave).
>
>**Step 3: Subtract the preparation time**
You need 1 hour of prep time before you leave.
>
>* 1:30 PM minus 1 hour = **12:30 PM** (This is when you must start preparing).
>
>**Step 4: Subtract lunch time**
You need 30 minutes to eat lunch before you start preparing.
>
>* 12:30 PM minus 30 minutes = **12:00 PM** (This is when you must start eating lunch).
>
>**Step 5: Check for conflicts with your morning schedule**
You have a 45-minute call scheduled at 11:00 AM.
>
>* **Call duration:** 11:00 AM to 11:45 AM.
>
>Since your call ends at 11:45 AM and you don't need to start lunch until 12:00 PM, your schedule works out perfectly with a 15-minute break in between.
>
>**Conclusion:**
>To make everything work, you should start your prep time at **12:30 PM** (with lunch starting at 12:00 PM right before it).
#### Comparison Analysis:
The direct, non-CoT prompt resulted in an incorrect answer (12:00 PM), confusing the start of the entire routine (lunch) with the specific start of the preparation time requested by the prompt. Conversely, the Chain-of-Thought output mapped out a clear linear timeline working backward, explicitly distinguishing between lunch, prep, and commute block boundaries.

CoT assists with complex reasoning because it forces the model to generate intermediate milestone states, acting like a scratchpad that prevents it from mixing up adjacent variables or steps in a sequence.

One observed limitation is that the model's accuracy heavily relies on the direction of logic it chooses; if it accidentally builds the timeline forward instead of backward, it can struggle to anchor the steps correctly to the rigid deadline of the 2:00 PM meeting.

#### Rephrased Prompt (Adding Constraints)
* **Constraint Prompt:** What are the main findings of Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 study on purple carrots?If you don't know, please say so rather than guessing
* **LLM Refined Response:**
>     "I do not know the main findings of Dr. Sarah Johnson's 2024 study on purple carrots, and my search did not return any records of a study matching that specific description."
>

**Takeaway Analysis:** Hallucinations are incredibly problematic because LLMs generate false assertions with the exact same confident, authoritative tone as real, historical facts, making them highly dangerous for users seeking objective truths. >
>Highly effective tech learners don't just memorize where buttons are; they look for the underlying architecture.

>* **First-Principles & Schema Thinking:** They look for universal patterns. If they are learning a new data tool like Tableau, they don't just memorize the steps to build a chart. ### 4.3 Limitations & Responsible Use Summary
When working with Large Language Models, users frequently encounter distinct limitations in factual accuracy, context tracking, and creative boundaries. Because LLMs generate text based on statistical probabilities learned from historical data rather than true understanding, they are prone to "hallucinations," where they confidently state false information as absolute fact. Additionally, they struggle with complex, multi-step logical reasoning and can easily lose track of nuances in long, intricate conversation contexts. Consequently, LLMs are entirely unsuitable for tasks requiring absolute factual precision, legally binding advice, or safety-critical decisions, such as medical diagnoses or formal financial planning. To use these tools ethically and responsibly in your work or studies, you must always independently verify all factual data against trusted primary sources, clearly disclose when AI has been used to assist in generating content, and actively rewrite prompts to filter out systemic historical biases.
